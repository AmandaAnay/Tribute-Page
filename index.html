CLEOPATRA VII

The controversial queen who led Egypt to ascension

Brief History

Queen's Origin and Family

Born in the city of Alexandria, Queen Cleopatra was descended from the Greek kings, the Ptolemaics.

The queen's ancestors were five hundred miles from Alexandria on the island of Filae. Over 300 years, temples dedicated to the XII Ptolemies were built in this place. Ptomoleus III was the last great pharaoh of the Ptolemaic era, he regained much of the wealth he had lost to other civilizations. Already Ptolemy IV failed and lost much of the riches of ancient Egypt.

Ptolemy XII, Cleopatra's father, was known as the flute player. He lived playing the small wind instrument, thus avoiding the government's responsibilities. At age 17, Cleopatra saw her father pass away. In the will left, Ptolemy XII said that Egypt should be ruled by Cleopatra and her brother, Ptolemy.

The Throne of Egypt

After her father's death in 51 BC, Cleopatra came to the throne of Egypt. As we have seen, Ptolemy XII left the kingdom to Ptolemy XIII and to Cleopatra, whom he was supposed to marry, according to tradition. However, the monarchs were surrounded by court men who wanted power and who had some influence over Cleopatra and her brother.

Queen of Egypt always understood that Rome was the new power in the Mediterranean and that in order to remain in power, she had to establish a friendly relationship with her. Then, in 49 BC Cleopatra offered the Pompey triumvirate (political alliance) and sixty boats to help fight Julius Caesar.

Faced with this attitude, the advisers raised the possibility that Cleopatra wanted to rule alone and then turned the people of Alexandria against her, who were forced to flee to southern Egypt and then to Syria.

Cleopatra and Julius Caesar

Cleopatra then decided to join an army of mercenaries and returned to Egypt to fight her brother. However, the international scene underwent a change and on August 9, 48 BC, Caesar defeated Pompey at the Battle of Pharsalia, in Thessaly. Defeated, Pompey sought shelter in Alexandria and Ptolemy XIII agreed to receive him.

Ptolemy XIII's real plan was to kill Pompey, believing that in this way he would please Caesar. Ptolemy XIII then hired a Roman to cut off Pompey's head, which was later presented to Caesar.

This attitude was seen as a mistake, as Caesar was appalled by the barbaric attitude. Despite political enemies, Pompey was married to Caesar's daughter, who died in childbirth. Thus, Caesar took Alexandria and decided to resolve the war between Ptolemy XIII and Cleopatra.

To resolve the crisis in Egypt, Caesar confirmed what he had in Ptolemy XII's will and confirmed Cleopatra and Ptolemy XIII as co-rulers of Egypt. Caesar further proposed that Cleopatra's younger brothers, Arsinoe and Ptolemy XIV, leave the country and become rulers of Cyprus.

Arsinoe was ambitious and had the army declare her Queen of Egypt. An officer began to oppose Arsinoe, she then had him killed and then her brother joined her.

In 47 BC the Egyptian army was defeated by Caesar. Arsione became a prisoner and Ptolemy XIII drowned in the River Nile while trying to escape.In June of the same year, Cleopatra's son with Caesar was born. Although he acknowledged paternity, Caesar refused to make the child his heir. The post belonged to Octavian.

The birth of Cleopatra's son brought benefits to Caesar. As the father of the heir to the throne of Egypt, he Caesar could guarantee the shipment of wheat to Rome without great financial loss. Julius Caesar and Cleopatra did not go up to the altar because Caesar was already married to another woman.

Relationship with Marcus Antonius

In Rome, the Second Triumvirate composed of Marcus Antonius, Octavius and Lepidus, organized the growth of Roman dominions towards the East. Marcus Antonius called Cleopatra for a meeting in the city of Cilicia. The Queen accepted the invitation and went in a boat all decked out in another and silver, to demonstrate the Egyptian power and wealth. Marcus Antonius fell in love with her, but returned to Rome to marry Octavia.

Marcus Antonius made Cleopatra queen of Syria, Cilicia, Cyprus, and Arabia. This act proved the wrath of Octavius who declared war on Cleopatra.

Leading a powerful fleet, Octavius and General Agrippa defeated Marcus Antonius, who made it to the shores of Alexandria and thought he was dropped by Cleopatra. In this way he committed suicide with his sword.

Besieged and slaughtered in the city of Alexandria, the queen decided to end her own life. Not satisfied, Octavius completely annihilated the line of succession of Cleopatra's heirs as well as turning Egypt into a mere province subordinate to representatives of the Roman power. Thus, the sign of loyalty represented by the military victory made Octavius the first Roman emperor.
